Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from them. They form when massive stars collapse under their own gravity at the end of their life cycle. The core of the star undergoes gravitational collapse, compressing into an infinitely dense point known as a singularity, surrounded by an event horizon, which marks the "point of no return" for anything falling into the black hole.

Black holes come in different sizes, ranging from stellar-mass black holes, which are several times more massive than the Sun, to supermassive black holes, which can have masses millions or even billions of times greater than the Sun. Despite their immense mass, black holes are incredibly compact, with their size determined by their mass and the radius of their event horizon.

Directly observing black holes is challenging because they do not emit any light. However, astronomers can detect them indirectly by observing their effects on nearby matter. For example, when matter falls into a black hole, it forms an accretion disk, which emits intense radiation as it spirals inward. Additionally, black holes can be detected through their gravitational influence on nearby stars and gas clouds, causing them to orbit around an invisible object.

Black holes have profound consequences on their surroundings. Anything that crosses the event horizon of a black hole is irretrievably drawn into it, leading to the destruction of matter. Black holes also have a significant influence on the evolution of galaxies, as their gravitational pull can affect the motion and distribution of stars and gas within them.
